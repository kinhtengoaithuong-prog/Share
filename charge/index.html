Charge+ vs. Segment C/D Petrol Cars

An overview of the Charge+ ecosystem, its competitive strengths by segment, and a fuel-cost balance simulator with a petrol-price slider for sensitivity testing. The real competitor is not other charging networks — it is the internal-combustion (ICE) car in segments C and D.

What Charge+ is

A neutral charging MSP operating across borders — not merely a station operator

Charge+ runs a Neutral Network (open model): it serves every EV brand — Porsche, BMW, BYD, Hyundai, Kia — unlike closed networks such as V-Green (VinFast only). It positions itself as a Managed Service Provider, not simply a seller of electricity.

The blunt read

Charge+'s strength is not energy price. As the Simulator tab shows, a driver relying 100% on premium stations saves only ~2% of TCO versus a petrol car — too thin to be a sales argument. The real edge is premium locations + a neutral network + a high-end B2B/B2C experience: customers pay for convenience and positioning, not for the cheapest kWh. Charge+'s arena is convenience/destination charging for the premium segment, not a price war against home charging.
The main competitor — clearly defined

The rival is the segment C/D petrol car, not another network

For a charging MSP, the market to open is the switch from petrol to electric. Other charging networks re-slice the existing EV pie; segment C/D petrol cars are the pie not yet cut. So the EV-vs-ICE TCO balance is the battlefield — and its most sensitive variable is the petrol price.

How to read the result

The petrol-price slider shows that the fuel balance is highly sensitive to the petrol price. When most charging is at home (3,200 đ/kWh), the EV wins decisively at any realistic petrol price. When reliant on station DC (8,000 đ/kWh), the gap narrows — and at low petrol prices the ICE can even match it. The sales lesson: the EV savings story depends heavily on charging behaviour, not just petrol price — which is exactly why Charge+ should lean on convenience and positioning rather than a per-kWh price war.
